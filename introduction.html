A State of Rest

Brandon Vickerd

Introduction to the exhibition

Welcome to A State of Rest, a public art installation created by Brandon Vickerd and situated along Strandherd Drive in Barrhaven, Ottawa. This installation features four life-size bronze animal sculptures: a fox, a fawn, a squirrel, and a raccoon. Each statue rests on a bench constructed from concrete, stainless steel, and iroko wood. This work invites you to consider the dialogue between natural forms and the stark modernity of urban materials. Here, delicate, realistically sculpted animals gracefully inhabit industrial seating, setting a tone of quiet contemplation in a busy public landscape. As you begin your visit, reflect on how this piece might prompt thoughts about the relationship between everyday urban life and the natural world around us.

Art Beyond Craftsmanship

Brandon Vickerd’s A State of Rest is far more than a display of technical skill or realistic bronze casting. While the craftsmanship is undeniable; from the intricate textures of fur to the anatomical accuracy of each animal, the deeper resonance of the work lies in its conceptual framework. This installation transforms a functional urban environment into a space of reflection, challenging viewers to consider the evolving relationship between humans and the natural world. 

At its core, A State of Rest offers a compelling commentary on environmental disruption, adaptation, and the uneasy coexistence between urban development and nonhuman life. By placing realistic, life-sized sculptures of wildlife in deliberately public, built environments, Vickerd creates a quiet tension between the natural and the constructed. The work blurs distinctions between artwork and audience, nature and infrastructure, visibility and invisibility. 

The presence of the sculptures invites interaction, yet it also provokes reflection. A passerby who chooses to sit beside one of the resting animals becomes part of the work’s unfolding narrative. This physical proximity challenges the viewer to consider their role not just in the scene, but in the broader story of how urban spaces are shaped, and whom they are shaped for.
