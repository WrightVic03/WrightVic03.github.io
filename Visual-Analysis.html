Visual Analysis

The Fox

Patina and lighting

The fox rests in a curled position, its form compact and peaceful. Sculpted in bronze, the surface carries a rich, natural patina that glows with deep amber and dark chestnut tones under direct light. Vickerd's meticulous attention to fur texture is particularly striking: each tuft is etched with fine, directional strokes that create a layered, almost tactile effect. The fur appears soft and dense, which is an illusion heightened by the warm highlights that catch along the ridges of the coat. 

Light plays a crucial role in the viewer's perception of the fox. Depending on the time of day and season, natural sunlight or snow reflection activates different aspects of the surface—casting soft shadows that deepen the dimensionality and catching highlights that emphasize facial features and contours. The sculpture responds to its environment, making every viewing experience slightly different, and reinforcing the idea that this is a living, changing encounter with nature. 

The Bench as a frame

Contrast and Containment

Vickerd also designed and fabricated the benches that support each sculpture, constructing them from concrete, stainless steel, and iroko wood. Their sleek, minimalist geometry forms a striking counterpoint to the softness of the animal figures. The benches are functional and architectural, clearly informed by urban design principles, yet they take on a sculptural role of their own. 

Rather than receding into the background, the benches contribute actively to the composition. They ground the animals in place—serving not only as platforms but as frames, accentuating the forms above them. The interplay between hard-edged, industrial materials and the organic fluidity of the animals creates a deliberate visual and conceptual tension. It is a juxtaposition that mirrors the broader theme of the installation: the coexistence of nature and modern infrastructure, and the friction that exists between them.

more than decorative

What may initially appear as a serene, even “cute” sculpture reveals deeper complexity upon closer inspection. The fox, with its detailed fur and contained posture, embodies the tension at the heart of the installation. It is both a symbol of rest and a reminder of intrusion—visually calm, yet conceptually unsettled. Through precise material treatment and strategic placement, Vickerd transforms this quiet figure into a subtle but powerful focal point of the broader conversation on urban life and ecological presence.
